# MealMutt Brand Voice

## The short version

We're a knowledgeable friend, not a brand. We did the research, we show our work, and we don't pretend to know more than we do. We talk to dog parents like adults. We're warm because we mean it — not because a style guide told us to be.

---

## Where we come from

MealMutt started because feeding your dog real food shouldn't require a veterinary nutrition degree or a $300 consultation. Commercial labels are opaque. Most homemade guides online are either dangerously vague or buried behind paywalls. We wanted something honest and actually useful.

That origin story matters for the voice. We're not a corporate product trying to seem approachable. We're a tool built by someone who got frustrated and decided to fix the problem. That's who we write like.

---

## How we actually sound

**Direct.** We say the thing. "Stop guessing what to feed your dog." Not "discover a better way to nourish your pup." If something doesn't pass an AAFCO check, we say it doesn't pass — and then tell you what to do about it.

**Specific.** "Roast at 350°F for 10–15 min, then grind fully before adding." Not "lightly roast and grind." Vague advice is a sign you don't really know. We know, so we're specific.

**Honest about the edges.** We check 5 nutrients. AAFCO has 37+. We say that — every time, in the same breath as the result. Saying "we check 5 of 37+" doesn't weaken our credibility, it builds it.

**Warm because of the subject matter, not the adjectives.** We're not "passionate about pet wellness." We're just actually interested in dogs eating well and we help people do that. The warmth comes through in how we treat the person, not from adding exclamation points.

**Firm exactly when it matters.** Safety warnings don't get softened. "NEVER use yogurt with xylitol — fatal to dogs." Short. No hedging. That's one place we don't worry about alarming people, because the alarm is the point.

---

## The things we've actively decided not to do

**Alarm people and leave them there.** When a nutrient is below minimum, we don't flash red and walk away. We state the finding and give two paths forward. Every warning earns a next step.

**Oversell what we are.** We're a cooking tool. We're not a vet clinic, not a supplement company, not an AAFCO certification body. We say this plainly. "We're a cooking tool, not a vet clinic" is one of our best lines — it sets expectations and builds trust at the same time.

**Use free as a threat.** We never frame the free tier as a limitation. "Your free plan only includes 3 recipes" is not how we talk. The free tool is genuinely useful by design. When Pro comes up, it's about more access, not about what you're missing.

**Hide the affiliate stuff.** Every affiliate link gets disclosed inline, right where the recommendation lives. Not in a footnote. Not in the privacy policy. Right there: "The Hilary's Blend link is an Amazon affiliate link — MealMutt may earn a small commission at no extra cost to you."

**Lean on dog puns instead of substance.** The name is MealMutt. That's enough. We don't need to call things "paw-some" or sign off with "woof." Personality comes from how we communicate, not from wordplay.

## Words we use (and why)

**Dog parents** — not pet owners. The people who use this tool cook for their dogs the way they'd cook for family. "Pet owner" sounds like a form field.

**Homemade meals** — not DIY dog food. DIY sounds like a weekend project. Homemade sounds like what it is.

**AAFCO minimum** — not AAFCO compliant. "Compliant" implies we've certified the whole thing. We haven't. We've checked 5 nutrients against minimums. That's what we say.

**Consult your veterinarian** — not "ask your vet." Small thing, but it shows respect for what vets actually are.

**Life stage** — not age group. It's the right term and it signals we know the field.

**Free** — just free. Not "free tier," not "free plan," not "limited free access." Free.

---

## Words we avoid

**"Simply" and "just"** — they imply things are easier than they sometimes are. "Simply add a supplement" lands differently when you're trying to figure out what to buy.

**"Premium"** — everybody says this. It means nothing now.

**"Revolutionary" / "game-changing"** — we're a recipe calculator. It's useful. It's not a revolution.

**Excessive exclamation points** — one per section, max. "You're on the list!" earns its exclamation point. "Fetching USDA data!" does not.

---

## How tone shifts by situation

The voice stays consistent, but the register adjusts.

The **hero** is the most confident we ever sound — because first impressions matter and we have to earn the scroll. Short sentences. Real problem. Clear value.

**Form inputs and labels** are neutral. No personality needed in a dropdown. Just be clear.

**Results and nutrition data** let the numbers speak. Our job there is to not get in the way.

**AAFCO checks** are matter-of-fact. State the finding. Give context. Offer a path. No drama, no celebration.

**Supplement copy** is educational with a firm edge on safety — because the consequences of getting it wrong are real.

**Upgrade prompts** lead with what you get, never with what you're locked out of.

**Errors** are short and helpful. Tell them what happened and what to do next.

**Legal pages** use plain language. Honest, not defensive. We actually mean what we write there.

## Real examples from the app

> *Stop guessing what to feed your dog. Get a recipe built specifically for their weight, age, and stomach — in 60 seconds. Free.*

Opens with the real frustration. Specificity signals we've actually thought about this. Free is the last word — not buried.

---

> *This recipe meets AAFCO minimums for protein, fat, calcium, phosphorus, and vitamin A (5 of 37+ nutrients). For complete nutritional balance, rotate proteins weekly and consult a board-certified veterinary nutritionist.*

We passed. We say so. Then immediately tell them what we didn't check. Then give them a real next step.

---

> *[Nutrient] is below the AAFCO minimum for your dog's life stage. This check covers 5 key nutrients — consult a veterinary nutritionist for a complete assessment. Consider adding a vet-approved supplement or adjusting ingredients.*

No alarm. Just the fact, the context, and two ways forward.

---

> *NEVER use yogurt with xylitol — fatal to dogs.*

Safety. All caps on NEVER. No softening.

---

> *MealMutt is not a substitute for veterinary advice. We're a cooking tool, not a vet clinic.*

This line does more trust-building than any claim we could make. Honest limitations are credible. Overclaims aren't.

---

## The gut check

Read it out loud. Does it sound like a real person who built something they care about? Or does it sound like a content team optimizing for brand consistency?

If it's the second one, rewrite it until it's the first.
